
Why I oppose the Howard Solstice High Voltage Line. I live in Fort Clark Springs, Texas. Our community is home to Las Moras Springs which is fed by the Edwards Aquifer. The Edwards Aquifer is one of the most hydrologically sensitive systems in NORTH AMERICA, and any large scale industrial intrusion into its Karst recharge zone is potentially CATASTROPHIC, both ecologically and socially.
Kinney County lies in the western “Devil’s River Segment” of the Edwards-Trinity Aquifer, which hydrologically connects the main body of the Edwards further east near Uvalde and San Antonio. 
The Las Moras Springs at Brackettville depend on the integrity of pressure differentials and subsurface conduits that recharge from limestone uplands north of Brackettville.
This spring system flows into the Rio Grande basin. Degraded quality affects BOTH domestic use and downstream ECOSYSTEMS. If transmission line construction introduces sediment or hydrocarbons, into the ecosystem, contaminants could emerge in WEEKS.
	1. Mechanical Disturbance
		Anchoring pylons require boring possibly 15-30 feet into 			bedrock.
		Vibrations can collapse small conduits or create new 				unwanted flow paths which potentially divert recharge 				AWAY FROM spring vents.
	2. Chemical Contamination
		Use of bentonite-based drilling muds, synthetic 							stabilizers, and petrochemical lubricants poses risk of 				direct infiltration. Even a small spill can have 								disproportionate effects in karst because the 								contaminates move unfiltered.
	3. Altered Recharge Dynamics
		Compaction of soils and construction pads reduces 					infiltration locally. 
		
	4. Noise and Microfracturing
		Seismic disturbances (from pilings or blasting) can 					destabilize cave ceilings and 
		aquifer conduits. This is especially problematic for bats 			and microfauna endemic
		to the system.

I would like to know if there have been hydrological risk evaluations, karst feature surveys, geotechnical borings, conduit mapping data, and contractor mitigation plans pertaining to the Kinney County segment of the HSHV Transmission project.
According to publicly available routing maps, the transmission corridor and associated drill sites would intersect karstic limestone terrain that directly recharges the Las Moras Spring complex. Planned construction involves subsurface boring and foundation anchoring into highly fractured Creataceous limestone units overlying the aquifer.

I respectfully OBJECT to this project. I have discovered in my research, that Kinney County and Val Verde County will not benefit in any way from this project. It is intended to move high capacity power east to west into the Permian Basin load centers. 
From an engineering standpoint, routing HV lines through karst recharge zones is indefensible. 
Karst is the most expensive and unpredictable substrate to stabilize towers on, and any cost savings gained by straight line routing vanish once sinkholes require remediation. Even more importantly, aquifer contamination or altered drainage is NON REPAIRABLE. The powerline route can be moved. However, a spring CANNOT be resurrected once the pressure differentials collapse.
I would like to see a board convened which unites hydrogeologists, karst biologists, and transmission engineers at one table. 
Please re-route this HV line around the recharge zone perhaps along Interstate 10 or existing railroads.
